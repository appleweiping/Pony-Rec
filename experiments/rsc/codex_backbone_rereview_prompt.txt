\subsection{Backbone Robustness: Two Additional LLMs Across All Eight Domains}
\label{sec:second_backbone}

The attribution above establishes \emph{which signal} carries the headline---the
task-grounded pointwise posterior---but not whether that signal is specific to
one backbone. To test this we re-run the \emph{identical} same-candidate pipeline
under two additional different-family frozen LLMs---Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and
Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3---changing only the scoring model and holding the
protocol fixed (10{,}000 users / Beauty $973$, 101 candidates, the same panels,
score schema, and seeded tie-break). This yields a full
$3\,\text{backbones}\times 8\,\text{domains}$ grid
(Table~\ref{tab:three_backbone}): a substantially broader robustness probe than
the single-backbone, few-domain checks common in LLM-for-recommendation work,
which typically vary only one backbone over a handful of representative Amazon
domains \citep{xu2025slmrec}.

\textbf{The ranking advantage survives frozen-model substitution.} Across the
six domains where the Qwen3-8B posterior ranks first over all eight official
baselines, Mistral-7B \emph{also} ranks first in all six, and Llama-3.1-8B in
five---the lone exception being Books, where the Llama posterior ($0.2710$) trails
the strongest baseline LLMEmb ($0.2737$) by $1.0\%$, which we report rather than
round away. In aggregate $17$ of the $18$ backbone$\,\times\,$(winning-domain)
cells clear the strongest official baseline---both per-backbone deltas are shown in
Table~\ref{tab:three_backbone}: Mistral improves on it by $+7.8\%$
to $+24.4\%$ and Llama by $-1.0\%$ (Books) to $+34.2\%$ (Electronics). Crucially,
the two domains where Qwen is honestly \emph{not} first (Beauty, $\#2$; Movies,
$\#5$) remain below the strongest baseline under \emph{both} additional backbones
as well, so the full \emph{win/loss partition}---six wins and two losses, not the
average---transfers (the added backbones are weaker in \emph{level} on the two
losing domains, so we claim transfer of the domain-level partition, not of exact
ranks or effect sizes). We also observe that the \emph{absolute} level tracks
backbone capability---Qwen3-8B strongest, Mistral-7B and Llama-3.1-8B typically
$\sim 10\text{--}25\%$ lower---an observation consistent with, though not proof of,
the headline being a \emph{ranking} advantage that a stronger frozen LLM expresses
more sharply rather than an artefact of any one model. We are deliberate about
scope: the official baselines are held at their unified-backbone comparison values
while only C-CRP's scoring LLM is swapped, so this establishes that \emph{C-CRP's
posterior signal stays competitive under frozen-model substitution}, not a
re-tuned cross-backbone SOTA result. The single sub-baseline winning-domain cell
(Llama on Books, $-1.0\%$) sits within the margin that per-seed scoring variation
can flip, so we rest the claim at the domain level---$17/18$ cells across two
unrelated model families---rather than on any one cell. The same pointwise scoring
code, score schema, and tie-break are shared across all three backbones, so each
substitution isolates the backbone as the only changed factor.

\textbf{A backbone-specific deep-recall diagnostic.} The replication also exposes
a model-specific failure mode that we measure rather than leave qualitative. The
Llama posterior cedes the deepest-recall metric (HR@20) to LLMEmb on several
domains. Grouping each $101$-candidate event, the Llama verbalized posterior
floors $84$--$90\%$ of candidates at exactly $0.0$, against $19$--$34\%$ for Qwen,
and floors the \emph{ground-truth positive} in $53$--$78\%$ of events (Qwen
$6$--$20\%$). A floored positive lands in a large zero-score tie block whose
expected rank pushes it past $20$, which is precisely a deep-recall loss; yet on
the $10$--$16\%$ of candidates Llama does grade it remains discriminative, so the
top of the ranking---NDCG, MRR, HR@5---stays sharp (hence the near-universal
NDCG@10 rank-1 result above). This also supplies the rationale for ranking by the \emph{raw} posterior
($\eta{=}0$): a risk re-weighting acts on the graded structure of the posterior,
and a backbone that floors $\sim 85$--$90\%$ of scores offers almost no graded
signal for such a term to exploit. This is a plausible diagnostic explanation, not
a re-derivation: it supports the conservative choice of ranking by the raw
posterior on the additional backbones and is consistent with the $\eta{=}0$-best
negative result of Section~\ref{sec:results}, but it does \emph{not} by itself
re-establish that result for every backbone; a full risk-instrumented $\eta$ sweep
per backbone is left to future work.
