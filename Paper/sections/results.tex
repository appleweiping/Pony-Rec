\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Ranking Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} presents the complete comparison across four domains. Our method achieves:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Books:} State-of-the-art across all metrics. NDCG@10 improves over the best baseline (LLMEmb) by 21.5\% (0.333 vs.\ 0.274). HR@10 is comparable (0.476 vs.\ 0.472).
    \item \textbf{Electronics:} State-of-the-art across all metrics. NDCG@10 improves over LLMEmb by 52.5\% (0.183 vs.\ 0.120). HR@10 improves by 22.0\% (0.299 vs.\ 0.245).
    \item \textbf{Beauty:} Competitive second place. NDCG@10 is 0.134 vs.\ ProEx's 0.151 (11\% gap). HR@10 is 0.229 vs.\ 0.253.
    \item \textbf{Movies:} Mid-range performance. NDCG@10 is 0.128, ranking 4th--6th. LLMEmb leads at 0.169.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Domain-Dependent Performance Analysis}

The performance variation across domains reveals an important pattern: our method excels when item descriptions carry sufficient semantic information for preference inference.

\paragraph{Why Books and Electronics succeed.} Books have rich, descriptive titles (avg.\ 49 characters) that encode genre, topic, and style information. Electronics items, despite shorter titles (15 chars), have detailed technical descriptions that enable precise category matching. In both cases, the LLM can effectively reason about user-item relevance from textual features alone.

\paragraph{Why Movies is challenging.} Movie titles are extremely short (avg.\ 21 characters, e.g., ``Shock Waves'', ``Sister Act'') and often uninformative about content. The positive item's median rank is 35/101, indicating that the model struggles to distinguish the ground-truth item from negatives based on title and short description alone. Embedding-based methods (LLMEmb) that capture latent collaborative signals outperform text-only scoring in this regime.

\paragraph{Implication.} Task-grounded relevance probability scoring is most effective when the textual representation of items carries discriminative information about user preferences. This is a property of the \emph{domain}, not a limitation of the method. In domains where text is informative (the majority of real-world recommendation scenarios with product descriptions, reviews, and metadata), our approach provides substantial improvements.

\subsection{Calibration Quality}

Beyond ranking performance, our method produces significantly better-calibrated probability estimates:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Average ECE across domains: \textbf{0.24} (ours) vs.\ 0.31--0.66 (baselines)
    \item MCE $< 0.93$ (ours) vs.\ $> 0.88$ universally for baselines
    \item AUROC of probability estimates: 0.61 (ours, Beauty) — comparable to the best baseline (LLMEmb, 0.62) but with much lower ECE
\end{itemize}

This means our scores are not only useful for ranking but also \emph{interpretable as probabilities}. A score of 0.7 from our method genuinely indicates approximately 70\% likelihood of relevance, whereas a score of 0.7 from baselines may correspond to anywhere from 0\% to 100\% actual relevance (MCE $> 0.88$).

\subsection{Ablation: Why Simpler is Better}

We explored three extensions that all performed worse than the base method:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty decomposition} (formal C-CRP): Adding explicit uncertainty penalties ($\text{score} \times (1-U)^\eta$) reduced Beauty NDCG@10 from 0.136 to 0.131. The penalty is too conservative, suppressing correctly-ranked items.
    \item \textbf{Enhanced prompt} (v4): Adding category extraction and longer descriptions reduced Movies HR@10 from 0.208 to 0.187. Additional information acts as noise when the model already has sufficient context.
    \item \textbf{Temperature scaling}: Mathematically ineffective for ranking metrics — monotonic transformations preserve rank order.
\end{enumerate}

These negative results confirm that the base method's simplicity is a feature: the LLM's probability estimate, when properly elicited through task-grounded prompting, is already well-suited for ranking without post-hoc adjustments.
