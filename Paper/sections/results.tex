Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports the main result across all eight domains,
and Figure~\ref{fig:improvement_bars} summarises it visually.
\method{} ranks first in six of the eight domains. Over the strongest official
baseline, it improves NDCG@10 by +21.6\% on Books, +53.2\% on Electronics,
+29.7\% on Sports, +32.2\% on Toys, +41.0\% on Home, and +43.3\% on Tools; in
these six domains the same pattern holds for HR@5/@10/@20, NDCG@5/@10/@20, and

\method{}). In the remaining two domains \method{} does not rank first. On
\textbf{Beauty} it is rank~2 of nine, trailing ProEx by $-11.0\%$ NDCG@10

shortfall is not Holm-significant on any of the seven metrics

indistinguishable from the strongest baseline there. On \textbf{Movies} it is
mid-pack---rank~5 of nine, trailing LLMEmb by $-24.2\%$ ($0.1281$ vs.\ $0.1690$)
and substantially behind: it is significantly behind the four stronger
baselines on 22 of the 56 paired tests. We report these two

per-domain per-metric improvement over the strongest baseline, including the
negative Beauty and Movies deltas, and Table~\ref{tab:ccrp_rank_by_metric}
gives the per-metric ranks.

The complete evidence ledger contains 72 method-domain rows: eight official
baseline rows and one \method{} row for each of the eight domains. Every row has
10,000 users (Beauty 973), 101 candidates per user, score coverage 1.0, and the

56/56; Books: 54/56), almost all deltas are positive and Holm-significant; the
only exceptions on Books are HR@20 versus LLMEmb (a significant loss) and HR@10

Holm-significant, while it is significantly ahead of the seven weaker baselines

\paragraph{Why the posterior wins on six domains but not Beauty or Movies: an exploratory decomposition.} To replace a qualitative guess with measurement, we relate the per-domain NDCG@10 gap between \method{} and the strongest same-candidate baseline to interpretable domain features---catalogue size, popularity skew (Gini and top-1\% interaction share), candidate-title textual homogeneity (mean pairwise TF--IDF cosine and token Jaccard over the 101 panel titles, and panel vocabulary size), and mean user-history length (Table~\ref{tab:gap_decomposition}; the two headline associations are plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:gap_decomp}). With only eight domains these are deliberately \emph{exploratory, descriptive correlations, not causal claims}, and no single feature remains significant once we account for the number of features tested; we therefore report the full feature table rather than a one-factor story. Three associations emerge. (i)~The strongest single continuous predictor is mean user-history length (Spearman $\rho=-0.69$, single-feature OLS $R^2=0.61$): \method{}'s relative advantage is largest where evaluation histories are \emph{shorter} and smallest on the two longest-history domains, consistent with directly elicited relevance helping most before a user has accumulated the long interaction history that ID/collaborative baselines exploit. We read this as a shorter-history \emph{tendency} rather than a cold-start regime, since mean histories span only $4.0$--$6.7$ items and Books is a winning domain with the second-longest history. (ii)~Candidate-text homogeneity moves inversely with the gap ($\rho=-0.24$ for TF--IDF cosine, $-0.45$ for token Jaccard): the two non-winning domains have the most textually homogeneous panels, where a per-candidate text posterior is least discriminative. (iii)~Contrary to our own prior conjecture, popularity skew does \emph{not} work against the posterior: the gap correlates \emph{positively} with skew ($\rho=+0.57$ for Gini, $+0.52$ for top-1\% share), \method{}'s margin in fact grows on more skewed long-tail catalogues, and Beauty---a non-winning domain---has the \emph{lowest} skew of all eight (Gini $0.26$). We state this reversal explicitly rather than retain the intuitive but unsupported ``loses on skewed catalogues'' reading. Finally, no single feature separates both non-winning domains, because they differ in kind: Beauty is a tiny ($1{,}184$-item), near-uniform catalogue where the dense-ID baseline ProEx has ample co-occurrence signal and the LLM posterior adds little---\method{} still beats LLMEmb there by $+9.3\%$, and its shortfall versus ProEx is not Holm-significant on any metric (Table~\ref{tab:significance_summary})---whereas Movies combines the most homogeneous candidate text with the longest histories and only $51\%$ item-title metadata coverage, a measurement limitation that itself handicaps a text-grounded scorer. (For Movies the history length uses an all-user interactions proxy because its $10{,}000$-user evaluation panel was pruned for storage; the released feature table flags this.) Taken together, the profile---larger margins on large, higher-skew, shorter-history, text-heterogeneous catalogues---motivates future hybridisation of the pointwise posterior with collaborative structure, and we offer it as analysis rather than a validated causal mechanism.

\paragraph{Attribution: the win is the pointwise posterior.} We are explicit
about which signal produces the headline ranking. The reported \method{} rows
in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} are obtained by ranking the 101 same candidates
by the raw task-grounded LLM relevance probability $\hat{p}$ alone---a pointwise
posterior per candidate---ranked descending with a fixed seeded tie-break. The
calibrated/uncertainty/risk-adjusted machinery is not what carries the gain.
Two pieces of evidence support this (computed on the four diagnostic domains
with full per-event signal rows). First, the leave-one-component-out summary
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation_ndcg10}) shows the uncertainty terms are inert or
redundant for ranking: boundary uncertainty is exactly inert in 4/4 domains,
and removing counterevidence or the risk penalty is nonworse in 4/4 domains on
NDCG@10. Second, a raw-probability attribution ablation
isolates the posterior directly. Holding the candidate rows, importer, and
seeded tie-break fixed, ranking by $\hat{p}$ alone tracks the reported \method{}
NDCG@10 within $\le 0.008$ across the three domains with local signal rows
(raw-$\hat{p}$ minus reported NDCG@10: $-0.0081$ on Sports, $+0.0024$ on Home,
$+0.0038$ on Tools). On the matched selector route, a calibrated
confidence-only variant that zeroes out the uncertainty and risk terms is
nonworse than the full configuration in at least 3 of 4 domains
(e.g.\ NDCG@10 $0.2287$ vs.\ $0.2281$ on Sports, $0.1374$ vs.\ $0.1364$ on Home,
$0.1711$ vs.\ $0.1695$ on Tools). We therefore attribute the headline result to
the task-grounded pointwise relevance posterior itself, and treat the
uncertainty decomposition as an analyzed---and for ranking, inert---design space
rather than the source of the gain. We make no claim that the posterior is
calibrated in the reliability-diagram sense; ``calibrated'' here refers to the
self-reported probability target of the scoring prompt, not to a measured
expected-calibration-error guarantee.

\paragraph{Reading the absolute scale.} Several official baselines sit close to
the random-ranking floor in Table~\ref{tab:full_official_ndcg10} (analytical
NDCG@10 $\approx 0.045$ for one positive among 101 candidates). This is expected
rather than anomalous. The unified protocol holds the backbone (Qwen3-8B), the
candidate set, and the score schema fixed for \emph{every} method, which removes
the native advantages of approaches whose value lies outside same-candidate
reranking: ELMRec is officially a T5 generative recommender bridged to Qwen3-8B
representations, and the LightGCN-profile methods ProEx/ProMax derive much of
their reported strength from full-catalog retrieval and popularity structure
that a 101-candidate sampled panel does not reward. Indeed, a pure popularity
ranking also scores at the random floor in each of the four new domains for
which we measured it (the \textit{Popularity} rows of
Table~\ref{tab:full_official_ndcg10}): because the sampled negatives are
themselves popularity-matched, the protocol deliberately neutralizes the
popularity shortcut, so a row near the floor is not unfairly penalized---it
simply fails to recover user-conditional relevance beyond a popularity prior.
We therefore do not rest the claim on the weakest rows. The informative
comparison is against the strongest baseline that is clearly above the floor:
this is LLMEmb in seven of the eight domains (NDCG@10 $0.094$--$0.274$) and
ProEx in Beauty (NDCG@10 $0.151$). On the six domains where \method{} ranks
first, it improves over the strongest baseline by $+21.6\%$ to $+53.2\%$
NDCG@10, and on the four new domains all 56 per-domain paired tests remain
positive and Holm-significant; on Beauty and Movies the strongest baseline is
ahead, as reported above. The result is thus a robustness claim under a fair
common-denominator reranking protocol, not a statement that these methods are
weak in their native full-catalog setting (see Limitation~5).

\paragraph{Bridge fidelity.} To confirm the unified Qwen3-8B representation
bridge is not itself responsible for the low baseline scores, we re-ran the
strongest SASRec-class baseline, LLM2Rec, in its \emph{native} encoder---an
LLM2Vec model over a Qwen2-0.5B backbone---on the identical same-candidate task,
holding the official LLM2Rec training and scoring fixed and changing only the
item-embedding source. The Qwen3-8B bridge recovers $110\%$ of native NDCG@10 on
Tools and $78\%$ on Sports (pooled $90\%$; HR@10 recovery $116\%$ and $93\%$),
and the bridged baseline stays well above the random floor in both domains. The
bridge therefore preserves most of a baseline's native same-candidate signal
rather than collapsing it, so \method{}'s margin reflects a genuine reranking
difference, not a bridge artifact. (We use a faithful community reproduction of
LLM2Rec, since the authors release code and data but no trained checkpoint, and
validate on the two domains whose native downstream task was prepared.) Two
further considerations bound the residual risk that the shared bridge
systematically weakens the baselines. First, Qwen3-8B is a strong general-purpose
$8$B encoder---generally at least as capable as the representation baselines'
native text encoders (e.g.\ LLM2Rec's LLM2Vec model over a Qwen2-$0.5$B
backbone)---so substituting it is more likely to \emph{upgrade} than to handicap
an embedding- or profile-based method, consistent with the $\ge\!90\%$ recovery
measured above. Second, the binding comparison in seven of the eight domains is
against LLMEmb, so a native-backbone fidelity check for LLMEmb specifically is the
single most informative remaining robustness test; we flag it as the priority
extension of this analysis (Limitation~\ref{lim:backbone} in \S\ref{sec:analysis})
and a full native-backbone reproduction of every baseline is otherwise left to
future work.

\subsection{Backbone Robustness: Two Additional LLMs Across All Eight Domains}
\label{sec:second_backbone}

The attribution above establishes \emph{which signal} carries the headline---the
task-grounded pointwise posterior---but not whether that signal is specific to
one backbone. To test this we re-run the \emph{identical} same-candidate pipeline
under two additional different-family frozen LLMs---Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and
Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3---changing only the scoring model and holding the
protocol fixed (10{,}000 users / Beauty $973$, 101 candidates, the same panels,
score schema, and seeded tie-break). This yields a full
$3\,\text{backbones}\times 8\,\text{domains}$ grid
(Table~\ref{tab:three_backbone}): a substantially broader robustness probe than
the single-backbone, few-domain checks common in LLM-for-recommendation work,
which typically vary only one backbone over a handful of representative Amazon
domains \citep{xu2025slmrec}.

\textbf{The ranking advantage survives frozen-model substitution.} Across the
six domains where the Qwen3-8B posterior ranks first over all eight official
baselines, Mistral-7B \emph{also} ranks first in all six, and Llama-3.1-8B in
five---the lone exception being Books, where the Llama posterior ($0.2710$) trails
the strongest baseline LLMEmb ($0.2737$) by $1.0\%$, which we report rather than
round away. In aggregate $17$ of the $18$ backbone$\,\times\,$(winning-domain)
cells clear the strongest official baseline---both per-backbone deltas are shown in
Table~\ref{tab:three_backbone}: Mistral improves on it by $+7.8\%$
to $+24.4\%$ and Llama by $-1.0\%$ (Books) to $+34.2\%$ (Electronics). Crucially,
the two domains where Qwen is honestly \emph{not} first (Beauty, $\#2$; Movies,
$\#5$) remain below the strongest baseline under \emph{both} additional backbones
as well, so the full \emph{win/loss partition}---six wins and two losses, not the
average---transfers (the added backbones are weaker in \emph{level} on the two
losing domains, so we claim transfer of the domain-level partition, not of exact
ranks or effect sizes). We also observe that the \emph{absolute} level tracks
backbone capability---Qwen3-8B strongest, Mistral-7B and Llama-3.1-8B typically
$\sim 10\text{--}25\%$ lower---an observation consistent with, though not proof of,
the headline being a \emph{ranking} advantage that a stronger frozen LLM expresses
more sharply rather than an artefact of any one model. We are deliberate about
scope: the official baselines are held at their unified-backbone comparison values
while only C-CRP's scoring LLM is swapped, so this establishes that \emph{C-CRP's
posterior signal stays competitive under frozen-model substitution}, not a
re-tuned cross-backbone SOTA result. The single sub-baseline winning-domain cell
(Llama on Books, $-1.0\%$) sits within the margin that per-seed scoring variation
can flip, so we rest the claim at the domain level---$17/18$ cells across two
unrelated model families---rather than on any one cell. The same pointwise scoring
code, score schema, and tie-break are shared across all three backbones, so each
substitution isolates the backbone as the only changed factor.

\textbf{A backbone-specific deep-recall diagnostic.} The replication also exposes
a model-specific failure mode that we measure rather than leave qualitative. The
Llama posterior cedes the deepest-recall metric (HR@20) to LLMEmb on several
domains. Grouping each $101$-candidate event, the Llama verbalized posterior
floors $84$--$90\%$ of candidates at exactly $0.0$, against $19$--$34\%$ for Qwen,
and floors the \emph{ground-truth positive} in $53$--$78\%$ of events (Qwen
$6$--$20\%$). A floored positive lands in a large zero-score tie block whose
expected rank pushes it past $20$, which is precisely a deep-recall loss; yet on
the $10$--$16\%$ of candidates Llama does grade it remains discriminative, so the
top of the ranking---NDCG, MRR, HR@5---stays sharp (hence the near-universal
NDCG@10 rank-1 result above). This also supplies the rationale for ranking by the \emph{raw} posterior
($\eta{=}0$): a risk re-weighting acts on the graded structure of the posterior,
and a backbone that floors $\sim 85$--$90\%$ of scores offers almost no graded
signal for such a term to exploit. This is a plausible diagnostic explanation, not
a re-derivation: it supports the conservative choice of ranking by the raw
posterior on the additional backbones and is consistent with the $\eta{=}0$-best
negative result of Section~\ref{sec:results}, but it does \emph{not} by itself
re-establish that result for every backbone; a full risk-instrumented $\eta$ sweep
per backbone is left to future work.

\Description{Line chart of mean NDCG@10 across four domains versus the C-CRP

\label{fig:observation}
\end{figure}

The observation module asks whether \method{} uncertainty is informative about
ranking reliability before using it as a decision feature. We compute
event-level uncertainty from the candidate-level C-CRP rows, bin events into
five uncertainty quantiles, and compare the lowest and highest bins under the
same ranking records used by the main metric importer. The package audit checks
1,010,000 finite uncertainty rows per domain, exactly 101 candidates per event,
exact candidate-key alignment, and score coverage 1.0.

Table~\ref{tab:uncertainty_stratification} reports the high-minus-low
differences for \method{}. Across all four domains, the high-uncertainty bin is
worse on NDCG@10, MRR, and HR@10. The largest gap appears on Toys
($\Delta$NDCG@10 = -0.3038), while Home and Tools still show clear NDCG@10
drops of -0.0690 and -0.0849. Figure~\ref{fig:observation} plots the full
per-bin trend averaged across the four domains, and crucially overlays the
LLMEmb baseline scored on the same events: ranking quality declines
monotonically from Q01 to Q05 for \emph{both} methods, so the uncertainty signal
stratifies same-candidate ranking reliability beyond \method{}'s own scores.

The module is descriptive. It does not show that manipulating uncertainty
causes a metric change, and it is not a substitute for the official-baseline
comparison in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}. Because events are binned by
\method{}'s own uncertainty and the ranking score is itself a monotone function
of the same posterior, part of this stratification is mechanical; we
therefore read it as confirmation that the uncertainty signal is monotonically
aligned with realized reliability, not as an independent causal effect. This is
consistent with the attribution above: the uncertainty signal is a useful
\emph{descriptor} of where the posterior is reliable, but adding it to the
ranking score does not improve same-candidate ranking.

\subsection{Component Ablations}

Table~\ref{tab:module_evidence} summarizes the paper-critical diagnostic
modules. The component ablation is especially important because it prevents a
too-clean story. Using removal-minus-full deltas on NDCG@10, boundary
uncertainty is exactly inert across all four domains (mean delta 0). Removing
counterevidence improves the mean by 0.00168 and is nonworse in 4/4 domains;
removing the risk penalty improves the mean by 0.00082 and is nonworse in 4/4
domains. Removing calibration gap is mixed (mean -0.00078; nonworse in 2/4
domains), and removing evidence support is directionally harmful but small
(mean -0.00013; nonworse in 1/4 domains). Figure~\ref{fig:component_ablation}
plots these removal-minus-full NDCG@10 deltas: every bar sits at or above zero
except the small calibration-gap and evidence-support dips, so removing a
component does not hurt same-candidate ranking.

The honest conclusion is that the same-candidate ranking gain is carried by the
task-grounded pointwise posterior, while the individual uncertainty components
are weak, redundant, or exactly inert under the frozen configuration. We
therefore report component ablations not as proof that every component is
necessary, but as a characterized negative result: the calibrated-uncertainty
and risk-adjusted ranking family, evaluated as ablations, does not improve
same-candidate ranking over the posterior it is built on.

Toys, and Tools; the maximum relative drop is $2.97\times 10^{-4}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:hyperparam_sensitivity} shows the $\eta$ sweep averaged across
the four domains: NDCG@10 is flat for $\eta\le 2$ and drops only at $\eta{=}4$.

The same result also limits the interpretation. The test-best $\eta$ is zero
in all four domains, so the paper must not claim that a positive risk penalty
is necessary or uniformly beneficial. The correct claim is stability and
sensitivity, not universal optimality.
