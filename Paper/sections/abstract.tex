\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for recommendation, yet the reliability of their scoring signals remains poorly understood. We conduct a systematic calibration analysis across 8 official LLM-based recommendation methods and 4 Amazon domains, all using the same Qwen3-8B backbone. We find a universal phenomenon: every method exhibits severe miscalibration (ECE 0.31--0.66, MCE $>$ 0.88), meaning their scores are informative for ranking but unreliable as confidence estimates.

Motivated by this observation, we propose task-grounded relevance probability scoring — a prompt-based approach that directly elicits calibrated probability estimates by requiring the LLM to ground its predictions in explicit evidence from user history. Without any fine-tuning or post-hoc calibration, our method achieves state-of-the-art ranking performance on 2 out of 4 domains (NDCG@10: +21.5\% on Books, +52.5\% on Electronics vs.\ the best baseline) and competitive performance on the remaining 2, while producing significantly better-calibrated probability estimates (ECE 0.24 vs.\ 0.31--0.66). Our results demonstrate that \emph{how} an LLM is asked to score candidates matters more than architectural innovations built on top of miscalibrated signals.
\end{abstract}
