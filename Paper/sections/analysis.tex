\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Why Same-Candidate Reliability Matters}

Many deployed recommenders use a multi-stage architecture: an upstream retriever
generates a candidate set, and a downstream ranker or reranker orders those
candidates. Our experiments target the second stage. This scope is narrower
than full-catalog recommendation, but it is also where LLM-based scoring is
most commonly used because evaluating every catalog item with an LLM is
expensive.

The same-candidate protocol makes this claim auditable. Since all methods rank
the same candidate rows, performance differences cannot be explained by a
different candidate generator. Since all methods are imported through the same
metric code, the comparison is not mixing native full-catalog metrics with
reranking metrics. This is the setting in which the uncertainty signal can be
evaluated as a decision feature rather than as a post-hoc explanation.

\subsection{What the Diagnostics Say}

The diagnostics support a careful interpretation. The observation module shows
that high uncertainty corresponds to lower ranking reliability, which justifies
including uncertainty in the framework. The component ablation, however, shows
that not every uncertainty component is equally useful. Boundary uncertainty is
neutral, and removing counterevidence or the risk penalty can be nonworse or
better on NDCG@10. Hyperparameter analyses reinforce the same caution: settings
selected on validation remain stable, but a positive risk exponent is not
required by the current evidence.

This combination is scientifically useful. It suggests that task-grounded
posterior estimation is strong under the same-candidate protocol, while the
exact decomposition of actionable uncertainty remains an open design question.
Rather than hiding the weak components, we report them as constraints on the
method story.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No full-catalog claim.} We evaluate controlled reranking over
    101 candidates per user. Candidate generation and full-catalog retrieval
    remain future work.
    \item \textbf{Single LLM and domain family.} The current formal comparison
    uses a unified Qwen3-8B backbone and four Amazon-domain same-candidate
    tasks. Other LLMs, catalogs, or retriever distributions may change
    calibration and uncertainty behavior.
    \item \textbf{Descriptive uncertainty diagnostics.} The observation module
    shows that uncertainty stratifies reliability, but it is not a causal
    intervention. It does not prove that changing uncertainty alone would change
    ranking utility.
    \item \textbf{Component necessity is not established.} Several uncertainty
    components are weak or redundant in leave-one-component-out diagnostics.
    The paper therefore claims that the C-CRP ranking framework improves
    controlled same-candidate ranking and that its uncertainty signal
    stratifies reliability, not that every decomposed term is essential.
    \item \textbf{External adapter policy.} We preserve official-code-level
    baseline mechanisms and use a shared Qwen3-8B representation bridge for
    same-candidate scoring. This gives a fair controlled comparison, but it is
    not the same claim as reproducing each method under its original full-catalog
    serving stack.
    \item \textbf{LLM cost and latency.} \method{} relies on task-grounded LLM
    evidence before score export. The current paper audits ranking quality and
    evidence integrity, but it does not optimize serving latency, token cost, or
    online batching.
    \item \textbf{Prompt and parser dependence.} The signal rows are
    fail-closed and source-audited, yet they still depend on the chosen prompt,
    output schema, and parser. Different prompt templates may change
    calibration and evidence-support behavior.
    \item \textbf{Sampled negative candidate sets.} The same-candidate protocol
    uses one positive and 100 sampled negatives per event. This is appropriate
    for controlled reranking, but conclusions may differ under larger or
    retriever-generated candidate pools.
\end{enumerate}
