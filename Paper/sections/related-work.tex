\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{LLM-Based Recommendation}

The integration of large language models into recommendation systems has
followed several paradigms. \textbf{Embedding-based methods} such as
LLMEmb~\cite{llmemb} extract item representations from LLM hidden states and
compute similarity scores. \textbf{Sequential methods} such as
LLM2Rec~\cite{llm2rec} and LLM-ESR~\cite{llmesr} combine LLM features with
sequential recommenders such as SASRec~\cite{sasrec}. \textbf{Profile-based
methods} including ProEx~\cite{proex} and ProMax~\cite{promax} use LLM-derived
profiles as preference signals. \textbf{High-order, representation, and intent
methods} such as ELMRec~\cite{elmrec}, RLMRec~\cite{rlmrec}, and
IRLLRec~\cite{irllrec} combine collaborative structure or user intent with
language-derived representations.

These methods differ in architecture and training objective, which makes a
fair comparison difficult unless the evaluation protocol is tightly controlled.
Our work treats these systems as official external baselines: each method keeps
its declared architecture and objective, while the data adapter, LLM backbone,
and score exporter are standardized so all methods are evaluated over the same
candidate rows.

\subsection{Calibration and Uncertainty}

Calibration of neural predictions has been extensively studied in supervised
learning~\cite{guo2017calibration,niculescu2005predicting}. Post-hoc methods
such as temperature scaling, Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999probabilistic}, and
isotonic regression~\cite{zadrozny2002transforming} can improve probability
calibration when a suitable calibration set is available. In the LLM setting,
prior work has studied whether models can express their own uncertainty in
words or probabilities~\cite{kadavath2022language,tian2023just,lin2022teaching}.

Recommendation adds a different challenge: uncertainty must be useful for a
ranking decision rather than only well calibrated as a scalar probability. We
therefore evaluate whether an event-level uncertainty signal stratifies ranking
reliability and whether risk-adjusted scores improve same-candidate
performance under paired tests.

\subsection{Uncertainty in Recommendation}

Uncertainty quantification for recommendation has been explored through
Bayesian approaches~\cite{wang2016bayesian}, ensembles, and conformal
prediction~\cite{angelopoulos2021gentle}. Much of this work focuses on model
uncertainty or prediction-set validity. Our focus is complementary: we construct
a task-grounded candidate-level posterior and study whether its uncertainty
components are actionable for reranking under the same candidate set.

\subsection{Same-Candidate Evaluation}

Sampled candidate evaluation can introduce subtle fairness risks if different
methods score different candidates or if full-catalog metrics are mixed with
reranking metrics~\cite{krichene2020sampled}. For this reason, our comparison
uses a same-candidate score contract: every method must emit a finite score for
the exact candidate key set, and all metrics are computed by a shared importer.
This does not replace full-catalog retrieval evaluation, but it makes the
reranking claim auditable.
